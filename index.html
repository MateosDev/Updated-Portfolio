I'm Oscar Mateos

Hi! My name is Oscar Mateos. I am a Web Developer, specializing in Front End Design. As a creator, I am naturally passionate and dedicated to my work. First impressions are tremendously important, therefore I find creating beautiful, responsive applications and websites to be a rewarding challenge; a task that empowers my clients to captivate their audience in a meaningful and visually stunning way. I enjoy every step along the way, from discussing ideas with my clients and collaborating with other developers to execution and product launch, but I get the strongest sense of satisfaction in knowing that the final product met and exceeded my client's expectations and needs.


Growing up in musically inclined family, I found myself participating in music since I was a young kid. I was gifted my first bass and electric guitar by my late grandmother, who shared her my love of music and it's ability to bring people together. Through the years, I enrolled in band which allowed me to strengthen my music theory skills while learning to play saxophone. In my middle school years I composed and arranged a few solo songs, later going on to play bass and guitar in different bands.Throughout this time, I also had a love for sculpture and ceramics, which I believe was what sparked my interest in design. Naturally, colors can draw certain emotions from us and can even be associated with different meanings, both positive and negative.


After high school, I attended Mediatech Institute, also known as Dallas Soundlabs for Music Production and Audio Engineering. It was there that I began to understand how technical and complex the process of making music was. It rekindled my desire to start putting out new music. While I was in school, I learned more about music theory, but the focus was more on the science behind music, and that drove me to become more interested in hardware and software utilized in the process. At the time that I was attending Soundlabs, music was going more digital or "in the box" than ever before. That meant that analog equipment was modulated by software that gave us unlimited capabilities.
